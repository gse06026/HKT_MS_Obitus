Unit 1: Ancient Civilizations - Key Terms

Civilization: An advanced stage of human social development and organization. Features often include cities, organized government, complex religion, social stratification, job specialization, and writing systems.

Mesopotamia: A historical region situated within the Tigris–Euphrates river system, known as the "Cradle of Civilization". Home to early civilizations like Sumer, Akkad, Babylon, and Assyria.

City-State: A political system where an independent sovereign city serves as the center of political, economic, and cultural life over its contiguous territory. Common in ancient Mesopotamia (e.g., Ur, Uruk) and Greece (e.g., Athens, Sparta).

Polytheism: The belief in or worship of more than one god. Characteristic of most ancient civilizations, including Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece, and Rome.

Cuneiform: One of the earliest systems of writing, distinguished by its wedge-shaped marks on clay tablets, made using a blunt reed for a stylus. Developed by the ancient Sumerians of Mesopotamia.

Hieroglyphs: The formal writing system used in Ancient Egypt, combining logographic, syllabic and alphabetic elements, with a total of some 1,000 distinct characters.

Dynasty: A line of hereditary rulers of a country or empire. Succession often passes from father to son. Common in ancient Egypt and China.

Primary Source: An artifact, document, diary, manuscript, autobiography, recording, or any other source of information that was created at the time under study.

Secondary Source: Information created later by someone who did not experience first-hand or participate in the events or conditions you’re researching. Examples include textbooks, scholarly articles, and biographies written after the subject's lifetime.
